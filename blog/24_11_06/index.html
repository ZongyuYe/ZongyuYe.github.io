Communication channel model between LEO satellite and Ground User

For the LEOS communications, the altitude, speed and position information of the LEOS are known to all BSs in a time slot. For the sake of simplicity, a quasi-static fading channel model is considered in a time slot. Thus, the signal received by the LEOS over the $Ka$-band is given by,

$$ y = \sqrt{p}h^{Ka}s+N_0, $$

$y$ is the received signal.

$p$ is the transmitting power from user.

$s$ is the signal from user with unit energy.

$N_0 ~ (0, \sigma^2)$ is the additive white Gaussian noise at the LEOs.

$h_{Ka}$ is the channel between user and the LEO. It can be given by,

$$ h^{Ka}=v\gamma d^{-\beta} $$

Here, we have,

$v~(0,1)$ is a complex Gaussian variable representing Rayleigh fading.

$\gamma$ follows log-normal distributed shadow fading.

$d_{ij}$ is the distance between user and LEO.

$\beta$ is the path loss exponent.

Then, the achievable capacity of user served by LEO over $Ka$-Band can be shown as,

$$ R = B_{ka}log_2(1+\frac{p|h^{Ka}|^2}{\sigma^2}) $$

$B_{Ka}$ is the bandwidth for one user.

Large Scale Fading

Here, Effective path loss can be shown as,

$$ PG_s = \sqrt{10^{0.1(PL_s+SF_s)}} $$

Shadow fading $SF_s$ is a random variable following the log-normal distribution, and its standard deviation is determined by the frequency, terminal environment, and the elevation angle.

The path loss can be expressed as,

$$ PL_s=A\cdot log_{10}d_s+B+C\cdot f_c +PL_A $$

where $d_s$ is the distance from the transmit antenna to the receive antenna of the snapshot $s$ and $f_c$ is the carrier frequency. $A, B$, and $C$ are the parameters determined by the environment, which are obtained by actual measurement. In the satellite communication environment: $A = 20; B = 32:45; C = 20.$

Atmospheric attenuation $PL_A$ can be expressed as,

$$ PL_A = \frac{A_{zenith}(f)}{Sin(\theta)} $$

where $θ$ is the satellite zenith angle and $A_{zenith}(f)$ is the attenuation value corresponding to different zenith angles and frequencies.

Distance Between Satellites on the Same Orbit

In the Starlink constellation, satellites are typically spaced about 5.45 degrees apart within the same orbital plane. Given that Starlink satellites orbit at an altitude of approximately 550 km, the distance between two adjacent satellites in the same orbital plane can be calculated using the formula for the arc length:

$$ d=rθ $$

$r$ is the radius of the orbit (Earth’s radius + altitude of the satellites, approximately 6371 km + 550 km = 6921 km)

$\theta$ is the angular separation in radians (5.45 degrees ≈ 0.095 radians)

Then, the distance is approximately,

$$ d=6921\times0.095\approx657\ km $$

Distance Between Satellites on Different Near Orbits

For satellites in different but nearby orbits in the Starlink constellation, a typical distance between satellites in adjacent orbital planes can be around 500 to 700 km. This distance can vary based on the specific configuration and phasing of the satellites, but this range gives a good approximation for typical spacing in such constellations.

The inter-satellite distance can be as close as 1 to 2 kilometers in certain segments of the constellation.

Inter-satellite links (ISLs) may offer higher speeds compared to ground-satellite links.

  1. Speed of Light in Free Space: Data transmitted via ISLs travels at the speed of light in a vacuum, which is faster than through optical fibers used in terrestrial networks. Optical fibers typically transmit data at about 65-70% of the speed of light. Also, ground-satellite links will be influenced by atmospheric interference.
  2. Higher Data Rates: Technologies like Optical Inter-Satellite Links (OISLs) can achieve much higher data rates compared to traditional Radio Frequency (RF) links, making them ideal for high-speed data transfer.
